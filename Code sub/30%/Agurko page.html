By Catherine Wong | cwong@times-standard.com | Eureka Times-Standard
PUBLISHED: May 29, 2018 at 6:57 p.m. | UPDATED: July 30, 2018 at 6:14 a.m.

Mechanical keyboards for PC gaming are becoming more and more of a necessity for “serious” and professional gamers, but at upwards of $100 the price can pack a lot of punch for some.

So, what do you do if you want all the advantages of mechanical keyswitches underneath your lightning-fast reflexes rather than the rubber membrane of your average keyboard?

Well, a number of things really. But even a keyboard without a numerical pad, arrows and function keys is still usually around $60.

Some opt to get just a mechanical number pad (those 18 keys grouped with Num Lock) because some games just don’t require that many buttons to play. But a numpad can hardly serve a proper keyboard.

So I present to you the Gherkin — a 30 percent printed circuit board designed to fit your extremely basic keyboard needs.

Keyboards are broken down in size by percentages. A regular or full-sized keyboard with 104 keys is considered 100 percent. If it’s missing just the numpad, it’s called an 80 percent or tenkeyless. If it’s also missing the function keys, it’s 60 percent.

At 30 percent, the Gherkin — which is actually narrow, elongated and, in my case, also green — contains just the 26 letters of the alphabet and an additional four keys.

The Gherkin was introduced by the online blog 40 Percent Club, which has since also introduced the Dilly, the Gnapkin and 25.

What’s deceptive about smaller keyboards is that they can’t function as well as their larger counterparts. For every group of keys missing, a function layer was likely programmed in. For example, by tapping one of those four additional keys to go “down” a layer, typing “QWERTY” could output “123456” instead. Then simply tap another key to go back “up” and continue typing as normal.

A Gherkin keyboard seems impractical at first. In fact, I brought my latest build into the office and was met with puzzlement. One co-worker looked straight at me and just flatly said, “Why?”

Well, size is a pretty obvious reason. A smaller keyboard means more desk space, and if I’ve learned anything from watching professionals play first person shooters, you really do want to aim with your entire lower arm, not just your wrist. Or, similar to a separate numpad, a Gherkin can also function as just a gaming keyboard.

Gherkin keyboards are also relatively cheap to build because of their size. Smaller board, fewer parts. Fewer parts, less overall material cost. Adding up my all my receipts and invoices that went into my latest build came to just under $32 — and that’s including LED lights and accessories, which make up around 25 percent of total cost.

How to build a Gherkin

The first step is ordering the PCB. The 40 Percent Club also has a step-by-step guide and video on how to have one manufactured for you, which means yours doesn’t have to be pickle green.

After that, it’s a matter of acquiring the bare minimum diodes, an Arduino Pro micro controller and switches. For LEDs, you’ll also want resistors and a MOSFET controller.

Once you have everything, solder all the diodes and resistors on one side of the board you want your switches to be on in their designated spots, keeping in mind which the resistors go in the right places and that diodes are polarized — meaning they’ll have to face a certain direction. On the opposite side, solder in the MOSFET controller, if you have one.

It is very important that you solder in the switches that will be opposite of the Pro Micro Controller before installing it, or you’ll likely never be able to do it again. (Unless you’re socketing the controller, but that’s jargon for another time.)

Then, it’s just a matter soldering the controller on and installing LEDs if you have them. And there you have it, a keyboard is born.

For finishing touches, you will need a USB micro cable so you can get to programming your function layers.

In terms of appearances, I did say earlier that the Gherkin is “extremely basic.”

Some kits and cases are available if you want to throw money toward them, but on my build, I simply opted to use a second Gherkin PCB and size M2 screws and spacers to protect the hardware underneath the typing surface.

It’s not the prettiest thing, but it’s mine.
